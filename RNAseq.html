what can we do

The scientist at BioinfoCore provide exhaustive RNA-seq analysis. We can work with our collaborators from experiment design to publication. Typically, the raw reads from a NGS are passed to our lab group for analysis.

Even todayâ€™s advanced sequencers make errors, and reads that hinder assembly or alignment need to be discarded. Depending on the experimental needs, the raw reeds are pre-processed to remove adapters, for trimming, and pooling of samples.

After a quality check, the reads are mapped to the reference genome with an aligner like TopHat2. If the data cannot be aligned to a well assembled reference genome, de novo assembly can be undertaken. The assembly process puts reads from the same transcript together until they cover the entire genome. For de novo assembly, the reads are built up into larger pieces then put together in the biologically most logical way.

If requested, our researchers can provide Differential Expression testing. This involves the identification of differentially expressed genes through statistical software packages like edgeR or DESeq2. These techniques identify both up regulated and down regulated genes whose counts are statistically unlikely to occur and hence have a very low p-value, even after correcting for false discovery. Both a complete list of interesting genes and useful visual representations can be provided to our collaborators. High resolution graphics such as Venn diagrams, clustering diagrams, and heat maps can be generated by our researchers from this data.

Sometimes the functional interpretation of genes identified by experiment are of interest. Our researchers can perform enrichment analysis and identify significant gene ontology within the sample.

We can provide download links to the analysis artifacts, including excel files, bam files, and images automatically generated by the processing pipeline. This data can provide our collaborators with the raw data to include with their publications, or as a resource for future experiments and analyses.
